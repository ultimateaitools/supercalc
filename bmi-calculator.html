What is BMI?

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a numerical value calculated from a person's weight and height. It is a screening tool used to categorize weight status and identify potential health risks associated with being underweight, overweight, or obese.

BMI Formula

BMI (Metric) = Weight (kg) ÷ Height² (m²)

BMI (Imperial) = (Weight (lbs) ÷ Height² (inches²)) × 703

Limitations of BMI

  • BMI does not distinguish between fat and muscle mass
  • Athletes may have high BMI due to muscle, not fat
  • BMI may not be accurate for elderly, pregnant women, or children
  • Asian populations may face health risks at lower BMI thresholds

Frequently Asked Questions

For adults aged 20 and older, a BMI between 18.5 and 24.9 is considered healthy. BMI below 18.5 is underweight, 25-29.9 is overweight, and 30 or above is obese. For Asian populations, the risk threshold starts at BMI 23.
BMI is a useful screening tool but has limitations. It does not account for muscle mass, bone density, body fat distribution, or ethnic differences. Athletes, bodybuilders, pregnant women, and the elderly may get inaccurate results from BMI alone.
To reduce BMI: (1) Create a caloric deficit through diet, (2) Exercise regularly (150+ minutes/week of moderate activity), (3) Eat more protein and fiber, (4) Reduce processed foods and sugar, (5) Stay hydrated, (6) Get adequate sleep (7-9 hours).
A BMI of 30 or above is classified as obese according to WHO standards. Class I obesity is 30-34.9, Class II is 35-39.9, and Class III (morbid obesity) is 40 and above. For South Asians, the obesity threshold is often set at BMI 27.5.
